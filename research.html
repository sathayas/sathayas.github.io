Research

With some background in brain imaging statistics and network science, I am interested in analyzing complex network data, often derived from functional MRI (fMRI). Here are some of the research topics that I am interested in.

Explosive percolation

I am interested in a statistical mechanics process known as percolation during network formation. Percolation is a phase transition phenomenon in which a large connected network (known as the giant component) emerges as edges are gradually added to a collection of isolated nodes. If the way we add edges is carefully orchestrated, a giant component emerges very abruptly, known as explosive percolation.

Anti-fragmentation

Some complex networks are often formed by thresholding a correlation matrix describing association between nodes (e.g., gene co-expression networks, fMRI connectivity networks). A widely used approach is to apply the same threshold to the entire correlation matrix, known as hard thresholding. However, hard thresholding often results in fragmented networks with a large number of disconnected nodes. One remedy for this problem is to threshold each row of the correlation matrix separately. This will result in networks with very few disconnected components.

fMRI network modules during a task

I am interesting identifying network modules associated with a simple fMRI task. A network module is a cluster of nodes that are highly interconnected among themselves but only sparsely connected to the rest of the network.

Statistical power calculation in fMRI experiments

In the past, I have developed a theoretical framework, based on random field theory, to calculate statistical power for fMRI experiments. This has been implemented in a software toolbox in MATLAB. Currently I am trying to port this toolbox into Python.
